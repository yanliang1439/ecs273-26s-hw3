Chevron is strengthening its long-standing position in Venezuela’s upstream sector through a strategic asset swap with state-owned PDVSA, aimed at concentrating its portfolio around high-value heavy crude assets in the Orinoco Oil Belt.
Under theagreement, Chevron will increase its stake in the Petroindependencia joint venture by 13.21 percentage points to 49%, bringing it close to a controlling position in one of the country’s key extra-heavy oil projects. At the same time, the Petropiar joint venture - where Chevron already holds a 30% interest - has secured development rights to the nearby Ayacucho 8 block, a producing asset that offers operational synergies due to its proximity.

Why is Chevron focusing on Venezuela's heavy oil assets?
What assets is Chevron gaining in this PDVSA swap?
In exchange, Chevron will transfer a series of assets back to PDVSA, including its operated interests in offshore Plataforma Deltana gas blocks and a minority stake in another western Venezuelan joint venture.
The transaction reflects a broader strategic recalibration by Chevron toward assets with near-term production upside and integration efficiencies. By consolidating its position in the Orinoco Belt - one of the world’s largest deposits of extra-heavy crude - the company is doubling down on projects where infrastructure is already in place and development risks are comparatively lower.
The Ayacucho 8 area is particularly significant. Its status as a producing asset adjacent to Petropiar allows for faster development timelines and reduced capital intensity, aligning with Chevron’s emphasis on disciplined capital allocation and incremental production gains.
Chevron’s move also underscores a renewed focus on Venezuela’s heavy oil sector at a time when geopolitical dynamics and sanctions policy have periodically reshaped the operating environment for international oil companies. While regulatory uncertainty remains a persistent risk, the company’s decision to deepen its exposure suggests confidence in the long-term viability of these assets.
The deal builds on Chevron’s century-long presence in Venezuela and reinforces its role as one of the few Western majors maintaining a meaningful footprint in the country. It also reflects a broader trend among international oil companies to optimize portfolios by shedding non-core or gas-weighted assets in favor of oil projects with clearer margins and established infrastructure.
Regionally, the transaction fits into Chevron’s wider Latin American strategy, where it maintains a diversified upstream portfolio spanning Argentina’s shale plays, Guyana’s offshore developments, and exploration acreage across Brazil and Suriname.
For PDVSA, the swap consolidates control over gas assets and certain upstream interests, potentially aligning with its domestic energy priorities and resource management strategy.
While financial terms were not disclosed, the asset exchange appears structured to deliver operational efficiencies rather than immediate cash proceeds, highlighting a long-term positioning play by both parties.
